Goal 1

Developing a Digital Tool

Motivation: During my studies I completed a coding course in which I created a digital maths learning tool for primary school children. This sparked my interest in how technology can create more personalised and engaging learning experiences. I find this topic challenging because integrating digital tools effectively requires both technical skill and a deep understanding of how children learn — especially in a diverse, international classroom where individual differences are significant. Positive feedback from my course tutor motivated me to explore this further in practice.

PDP Goal: I will develop and implement a digital maths learning tool that supports children in practising basic numeracy skills independently in order to create a learning environment that motivates individual learners and responds to individual differences.

Theory: • Herset, El Ghami & Bjerke (2023)- this study found that adjusting maths tasks by difficulty level significantly affects students' self-efficacy. This directly supports the three-level structure of my tool (one-digit, two-digit, three-digit numbers), where clearly labelled levels help children find their appropriate starting point and experience success before moving up. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1116386 • Ersozlu (2024)- this literature found that interactive digital tools and game-based learning show positive results in reducing mathematics anxiety and building positive attitudes toward maths in primary school children- supporting the use of my tool as an accessible, low-pressure practice environment. cedtech.net • Engelbrecht & Borba (2024)- recent developments in digital technology in mathematics document how digital tools are increasingly used to enable personalised learning and provide new ways for children to engage with mathematical concepts. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11858-023-01530-2 • Zhou (2024)- this study confirms that scaffolding is most effective when learning tasks are structured to match each student's current level, and that differentiation by both content and process supports all learners in a mixed-ability classroom. spark.bethel.edu

Practice: Before implementing the tool, I conducted small-scale field research in both my EY3 and Grade 3 classrooms to understand how children currently practise maths independently and where differentiation gaps existed. In EY3, I observed that children at this age are beginning to explore one-digit addition and subtraction, but their concentration spans during independent tasks are short. I spoke with my mentor teacher, who confirmed that children in this group vary significantly in their number sense. While some are still consolidating counting, while others are ready to begin simple operations. This suggested that Level 1 (one-digit numbers) would be the appropriate starting point, used in short and guided sessions during free-choice or structured activity time. In Grade 3, I reviewed student work and recent assessment data with my mentor teacher. Most children were confident with one- and two-digit operations, but a smaller group was ready to be challenged with three-digit numbers, while a handful were still consolidating two-digit subtraction with borrowing. This confirmed that all three levels would be needed simultaneously within the same class, and that allowing children to self-select their level, with teacher guidance, to support both independence and appropriate challenge (Herset et al., 2023). I also spoke with a more experienced colleague, who emphasised the importance of introducing the tool explicitly before independent use and making its purpose clear to children, so they understand why they are using it.

Interventions: Based on my field research and the theory above, I chose the following interventions: • Explicit introduction of the tool (both groups): I introduced the tool through a whole-class demonstration, modelling how to choose one of the four operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) and a number level, and explaining the purpose of the activity. This ensured children could use it independently and understood its learning goal (Zhou, 2024). • Level assignment with guided choice (Grade 3): Based on my review of assessment data, I guided each child toward a starting level matching their current ability. Children were encouraged to move up a level when they felt ready, supporting learning autonomy and self-efficacy (Herset et al., 2023). • Short, structured sessions (EY3): With EY3 children, I used the tool in short five-to-ten minute sessions at Level 1 only, always with adult support nearby. I modelled thinking aloud during operations to make the mathematical reasoning visible (Ersozlu, 2024). • Independent practice station (Grade 3): In Grade 3 the tool was set up as one of several independent work stations during maths lessons, allowing children to practise at their own pace and level while I worked with small groups, supporting a well-organised, purposeful classroom environment (Engelbrecht & Borba, 2024). • Reflective log: Throughout the implementation I kept a reflective log after each session, noting what worked, what was challenging, and what I would adapt.
