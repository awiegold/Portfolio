Goal 3

Collaboration

Motivation: As I approach the end of my study, I want to grow as a professional who contributes confidently to the school community and not just in the classroom but collaborating with colleagues and parents. In my school, team meetings take place every Wednesday and cover both housekeeping and curriculum development. I have also had opportunities to communicate with parents through sharing an Approaches to Learning (ATL) skill rubric and Toddle post about classroom learning.

PDP Goal: I aim to develop my professional collaboration skills by actively engaging with colleagues, parents, and relevant professionals within the diverse school community. I will contribute to at least three team meetings, participate in at least two communication moments with parents or guardians, and request and reflect on feedback from at least two colleagues.

Theory:

• Epstein (2001)- Epstein's study identifies communication as one of the six key types of involvement that strengthen the school-family partnership. Sharing ATL rubrics and Toddle posts with parents is a concrete way of practising this type of transparent, accessible partnership communication. https://us.sagepub.com/sites/default/files/upm-assets/25536_book_item_25536.pdf

• Korthagen (n.d.)- Promoting Core Reflection: seeking feedback from colleagues is a key step in the reflective cycle. By deliberately requesting and reflecting on feedback, I can make my professional growth more systematic and intentional rather than incidental. korthagen.nl

Practice: To prepare for this goal, I began by observing how my colleagues engage in the Wednesday team meetings. I noticed that the most effective contributions seemed to be about classroom experience, e.g. sharing an observation about a child's learning, raising a question about curriculum planning, or suggesting a practical solution to an organisational challenge. This helped me understand what meaningful participation looks like in this context. I also reflected on my existing parent communication practice. Early in the school year, once children had had time to settle in, I developed and shared an ATL skill rubric with parents. This gave families a structured and accessible picture of how their child was settling into school. I also used Toddle to share regular posts with parents about what the class is working on, including photos of activities and brief explanations of the learning intentions. These kept parents connected to classroom life in an ongoing way between more formal communication moments.

Interventions:

Based on my observations, mentor conversations and the theory above, I implemented the following structured daily routine:

• Active contribution to Wednesday team meetings: Rather than listening passively, I prepared at least one contribution for each Wednesday meeting- either a classroom observation, a question about curriculum planning, or a suggestion related to school organisation.

• Sharing ATL skill rubrics with parents: After children had settled into the school year, I developed and shared an ATL skill rubric with parents covering some of the Approaches to Learning. The rubric was designed to be clear and accessible so that parents from all language backgrounds could understand their child's progress (Epstein, 2001).

• Toddle posts to communicate learning: I used Toddle to share regular posts with parents about what the class is working on, including photos of activities and brief explanations of the learning intentions. These kept parents connected to classroom life and supported the school-family partnership (Epstein, 2001).

• Requesting peer feedback: I asked two colleagues to observe me during a typical Wednesday meeting and to give me feedback on my participation.
