Goal 2

Supporting Social Skills

Motivation: Working with young children in EY1 and EY2, I noticed that many students struggled with cooperative play, particularly around sharing toys, resolving conflicts without physical responses such as hitting, and understanding the effect of their behaviour on others. In a diverse, international early years setting, different cultural norms around social interaction can mean that children come to school with very different understandings of what cooperative behaviour looks like. I was motivated to develop a structured, visual and age-appropriate approach to help children build these skills in a safe and consistent way.

PDP Goal: I will guide students in developing cooperative play and group interaction skills through structured visual tools and guided reflection moments, fostering autonomy, respect, and a sense of belonging within our diverse classroom community.

Theory:

• Aksoy & Gresham (2024)- this study confirms that structured, concrete tools such as picture-based activities, puppets and visual stories are among the most effective methods for developing social-emotional skills in preschool children. The review found that these approaches are particularly powerful when combined with explicit discussion and reflection, directly supporting the picture card and poster system used in this intervention. https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1437131.pdf

• Korthagen (n.d.)- Korthagen's paper on the ALACT model describes the five-phase reflective cycle- action, looking back, awareness of essential aspects, creating alternative methods, and trial. The daily circle routine in this intervention follows this cycle directly: morning play (action), circle time discussion (looking back and awareness), generating alternative behaviours (creating alternatives), and applying these in the next play session (trial). Available on the author's website: korthagen.nl

• Vygotsky (1978) — Zone of Proximal Development: by scaffolding reflection through visual tools rather than abstract language, I make social learning accessible for very young children who are still developing the vocabulary to describe and reflect on social situations. The picture cards act as a concrete bridge between experience and understanding

Practice: Before developing the intervention, I observed children during morning free play over several days to identify recurring patterns of behaviour. I noticed the following recurring situations: children grabbing toys from each other rather than waiting for a turn; physical responses such as pushing or hitting when conflicts occurred; children struggling to ask for help or to communicate their needs to peers or adults. I spoke with my colleague about these observations. She confirmed that these were consistent patterns she had also noticed, particularly during unstructured play time. She noted that previous attempts to address this verbally during circle time had limited impact because the children struggled to connect abstract discussion to their actual behaviour. She than shared that visual tools tend to be significantly more effective with this age group than verbal discussion alone because children at EY1 and EY2 level are still developing the language to describe and reflect on social situations. This confirmed my idea to use picture cards as the basis for the intervention and is consistent with the findings of Aksoy and Gresham (2024). I developed a set of picture cards showing both positive behaviours (sharing a toy, helping a friend, talking to a teacher when upset, taking turns) and negative behaviours (throwing toys, hitting, grabbing, shouting). These were designed to reflect situations I had actually observed in the classroom, making them directly meaningful to the children.

Interventions:

Based on my observations, mentor conversations and the theory above, I implemented the following structured daily routine: • Morning play observation: Each morning during free play, I observe the children carefully and note specific behaviours, both positive and negative. I select the picture cards that correspond to what I have seen that morning, choosing a balanced mix where possible (Aksoy & Gresham, 2024).

• Green and red poster sorting (circle time): At the start of morning circle, I present the selected picture cards one by one and ask children what they can see on the picture and where each one should go, on the green poster (positive behaviours we want to see more of) or the red poster (behaviours we want to avoid). Children are invited to explain their reasoning, supporting language development and moral awareness (Vygotsky, 1978).

• Discussion of red poster behaviours: For each card placed on the red poster, we discuss together: why this behaviour can hurt others, and what we could do instead. For example, when discussing hitting, we talk about using words to say how we feel or going to a teacher for help. This directly addresses the "creating alternative methods" step of the ALACT cycle (Korthagen, n.d.).

• Setting a group intention: At the end of the circle discussion, we look at both posters together and set a shared intention: "Today we want to fill the green poster." This gives children a concrete, visual goal and a sense of shared responsibility for the classroom community.

• Reminders: During play sessions, I refer to the posters when a conflict arises- for example: "Remember what we said about the red poster? What could we do instead?" This bridges the circle time reflection to real behaviour in context, completing the ALACT trial phase (Korthagen, n.d.).

Evidence: Evidence of planning • Photos of the green and red poster system. Evidence of action • Observation notes from free play sessions. Evidence of feedback • Mentor teacher written feedback.
